# **The Institutional Resilience of FactSet Research Systems: A Deep Value Examination of Financial Data Infrastructure in the Algorithmic Age**

The global financial information services industry stands at a critical juncture, characterized by a stark divergence between the perceived obsolescence of traditional data terminals and the actual, quantifiable growth of integrated financial intelligence. FactSet Research Systems (FDS), a stalwart of the sector for over four decades, currently embodies this tension. While the company continues to report record revenues, expanding client counts, and industry-leading retention rates, its equity valuation has compressed to levels historically reserved for distressed or declining enterprises. This report investigates the fundamental health, competitive durability, and intrinsic value of FactSet to determine whether the current market pricing represents a rational adjustment to technological disruption or a profound miscalculation by a market focused on short-term sentiment rather than long-term compounding power.

## **1\. Financial Health and Operational Sustainability**

The assessment of FactSet's financial strength requires a longitudinal view of its performance across varying economic cycles, including the global financial crisis, the sovereign debt crises of the early 2010s, and the recent period of high inflation and rising interest rates.

### **A. Profitability and Earnings Continuity**

FactSet’s earnings trajectory is defined by a level of consistency that is exceptional within the technology and capital markets sectors. The company has achieved 45 consecutive years of revenue growth and 29 consecutive years of adjusted earnings per share (EPS) growth.1 This record is not merely a statistical anomaly but a reflection of a business model built on recurring, high-margin subscription revenue.

Net income trends over the last decade show a steady upward march. In fiscal year 2011, annual net income was approximately $171 million.2 By the end of fiscal 2025, this figure reached $597 million, representing a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of approximately 9.3% over the 14-year period.2 Quarterly performance in fiscal 2025 and early fiscal 2026 further validates this trend, with Q1 2026 reporting net income of $150 million, a 4.4% increase in diluted EPS compared to the prior year period.4

Profit margins remain a core strength, although they have experienced slight compression due to the integration of the CUSIP Global Services (CGS) acquisition and increased investment in artificial intelligence. For the trailing twelve months ended November 30, 2024, FactSet maintained a net profit margin of 25.40% and an operating margin of 32.01%.7 While GAAP operating margins dipped from 33.6% in Q1 2025 to 31.6% in Q1 2026, the adjusted operating margin, which excludes non-recurring items and acquisition-related amortization, remained robust at 36.2%.5 This indicates that the core business continues to operate with significant efficiency, even as it absorbs the costs of long-term strategic investments.

Return on Equity (ROE) remains a critical metric for evaluating management’s ability to generate value from shareholder capital. FactSet’s ROE has historically been among the highest in its peer group, though it has trended downward as the equity base expanded following the CUSIP acquisition. The current ROE stands at 28.8%, compared to a 5-year average of 33.4%.8 This decline is a mathematical function of a larger equity denominator rather than a loss of earnings power, as net income continues to reach new highs.8

### **B. Debt Levels and Leverage Analysis**

FactSet’s capital structure underwent a significant shift in 2022 with the $1.9 billion acquisition of CGS. This necessitated the incurrence of substantial debt, which management has been methodically reducing. As of November 30, 2025, total debt was approximately $1.37 billion.11 The debt-to-equity ratio currently stands at 63.1%, which is elevated relative to FactSet’s historical debt-free status but remains manageable given the company's strong cash flows.11

The interest coverage ratio is a vital indicator of financial health, especially in a fluctuating interest rate environment. FactSet’s EBIT-to-interest expense ratio is currently 15x, providing a substantial cushion against rising rates.11 The debt is primarily composed of long-term obligations, with current debt (maturing within one year) standing at only $62.5 million.4 The company’s leverage ratio (Total Debt/EBITDA) has improved to approximately 1.5x, reflecting disciplined repayment and EBITDA growth.13

### **C. Cash Flow and Capital Allocation**

The transition from net income to free cash flow (FCF) is the ultimate test of earnings quality for a value investor. FactSet demonstrates exceptional cash conversion. In fiscal 2025, the company generated $726.3 million in operating cash flow, leading to $617.5 million in free cash flow after accounting for $108.8 million in capital expenditures.3 This FCF represented 26.6% of total revenue, a margin that highlights the asset-light nature of its digital distribution model.

CapEx trends show a slight increase as FactSet invests in cloud migration and AI infrastructure, but these costs are well within the company’s ability to self-fund. The cash conversion cycle is further optimized by the fact that FactSet typically bills its clients in advance, resulting in a favorable working capital position and a significant deferred revenue liability (currently $157 million) that essentially acts as an interest-free loan from its customers.4

### **D. Balance Sheet Strength and Efficiency**

FactSet’s balance sheet is characterized by high asset quality, though it is heavily weighted toward intangibles following its history of acquisitions. Total assets of $4.3 billion include approximately $2.96 billion in goodwill and intangible assets.3 While some investors view high goodwill with skepticism, FactSet’s high ROIC suggests that these acquisitions have been value-accretive rather than dilutive.

The current ratio stands at 1.54x, and the quick ratio is similarly healthy, as the company carries no physical inventory.11 Working capital trends have remained positive over the last five years, supporting the company's liquidity needs for both operational reinvestment and shareholder returns.

## **2\. Valuation Metrics: The Deep Value Opportunity**

The primary argument for FactSet as a deep value play rests on the massive compression of its valuation multiples relative to both its own history and its current fundamental performance.

### **A. Historical vs. Current Price Ratios**

As of early February 2026, FactSet’s stock price of approximately $205.79 implies a trailing P/E ratio of 12.7x to 13.3x.14 This is a startling departure from historical norms. Over the last ten years, FactSet’s average P/E ratio has been 30.71x, with a high of 43.55x in late 2021\.18 The current multiple represents a 58% discount to the ten-year average. Even more compelling is the fact that the current P/E is lower than any point in the last 15 years, including the market lows of the 2016 and 2020 pullbacks.18

The Enterprise Value to EBITDA (EV/EBITDA) multiple confirms this deep undervaluation. FactSet currently trades at approximately 9.1x to 10.1x EV/EBITDA.20 The historical median for this metric is 21.52x, indicating that the market is valuing FactSet's earnings power at less than half of its traditional worth.21

### **B. Dividend Analysis and Yield Support**

FactSet has maintained its status as a premier dividend growth company, with 26 consecutive years of increases.1 In May 2025, the company raised its quarterly dividend by 6% to $1.10 per share.3 At a current share price of $205.79, the forward dividend yield is 2.14%.14 This yield is nearly double its 10-year historical average of approximately 1.1%, providing a formidable yield-on-cost opportunity for long-term investors.

The payout ratio is highly conservative, with dividends consuming only 28% of net income and approximately 27% of free cash flow.16 This suggests that even in a scenario where growth temporarily stalls, the dividend is extremely secure and has significant room for continued expansion.

## **3\. Business Quality and the Economic Moat**

FactSet’s durability is derived from its deep integration into the critical daily workflows of the global investment community. The company does not simply provide data; it provides the infrastructure upon which investment decisions are made, measured, and reported.

### **A. The Three Pillars of the Moat**

The "economic moat" of FactSet is characterized by high switching costs, proprietary data assets, and a "land and expand" customer relationship model.

1. **High Switching Costs and Workflow Integration:** For an institutional asset manager or investment banker, the FactSet terminal and its associated APIs are deeply embedded in proprietary valuation models, risk management frameworks, and client reporting systems. Replacing FactSet requires not just a change of software, but a massive overhaul of internal data architecture and the retraining of highly paid staff. This results in a "sticky" relationship, evidenced by a client retention rate that has remained at 91% and an ASV retention rate exceeding 95% for over a decade.1
2. **Proprietary Data and the CUSIP Monopoly:** Through the acquisition of CUSIP Global Services (CGS), FactSet gained control of the unique identification system for nearly all North American financial instruments. This is a non-discretionary utility; if a firm trades, clears, or settles a US security, it must interact with CUSIP data.13 This provides FactSet with a high-margin, regulatory-adjacent revenue stream that is virtually impossible for a competitor to replicate.
3. **Trust and Data Integrity:** In an era of "hallucinating" AI and fragmented data sources, FactSet’s reputation for "clean," curated, and audited data is a competitive advantage. The company has spent over 45 years cleaning and mapping complex financial datasets, creating a "data lake" that serves as a single source of truth for firms that cannot afford errors in their calculations.23

### **B. Market Position and Competitive Landscape**

FactSet occupies a unique "sweet spot" in the market. While Bloomberg remains the dominant player in the trading floor segment (with a 33.4% market share), FactSet has established itself as the preferred provider for the "buy-side" (asset managers and hedge funds) and "wealth management" segments.25

The institutional buy-side accounts for 82% of FactSet’s organic ASV, a segment where deep analytics and portfolio construction tools are more critical than the real-time news and chat features that drive Bloomberg’s dominance.10 FactSet's ability to maintain and even grow its client count (reaching 9,003 firms in late 2025\) suggests that it is successfully defending its niche while expanding into adjacent areas like wealth management and market infrastructure.5

### **C. Management and Capital Allocation**

The leadership transition from Phil Snow (who led the company for a transformative decade) to Sanoke Viswanathan in September 2025 marks a strategic pivot toward AI and digital transformation.28 Viswanathan’s background as a Chief Strategy Officer at JPMorgan Chase and a partner at McKinsey suggests a focus on operational efficiency and scaling high-growth segments like international wealth management.29

Capital allocation history demonstrates a disciplined approach:

* **Dividends:** 26 years of consecutive increases, prioritizing shareholder income.1
* **Share Buybacks:** Management has historically used buybacks as a secondary return mechanism, but recently accelerated this by authorizing a $1 billion program, signaling their view that the shares are significantly undervalued.5
* **M\&A Strategy:** FactSet has avoided "transformational" mega-deals in favor of "tuck-in" acquisitions like LiquidityBook and Irwin, which enhance specific product capabilities (OMS and Investor Relations) without over-leveraging the balance sheet.27

### **D. Revenue Quality and Visibility**

FactSet’s revenue is 100% recurring in nature. The company recently transitioned its reporting to focus on "Organic ASV" (Annual Subscription Value), which represents the forward-looking revenues for the next 12 months from all current subscription services.1 This provides investors with extreme visibility into future cash flows. Unlike transactional businesses that suffer during market volatility, FactSet’s revenue remains stable as long as firms remain in business and keep their terminals active.

## **4\. Growth and Future Prospects: Beyond the Terminal**

The primary bear case for FactSet is that AI will replace financial analysts, thereby shrinking the company's "user count" and terminal revenue. However, the evidence suggests that FactSet is leveraging AI to expand its Total Addressable Market (TAM) and increase the value it provides per user.

### **A. The AI Opportunity: AigenTeq and Automation**

FactSet is not a passive bystander in the AI revolution. Through its "AigenTeq" initiative and other GenAI integrations, the company is automating the most monotonous tasks of financial analysis.

* **Research Automation:** AI tools now enable analysts to process earnings calls and regulatory filings in minutes rather than hours, allowing them to cover more companies with higher precision.27
* **Wealth Management Tools:** New AI-powered prospecting solutions help wealth managers identify high-net-worth clients more effectively, driving the 10% growth seen in the wealth segment in fiscal 2025\.27
* **Private Capital Ingestion:** AI Doc Ingest for the Cobalt platform transforms how private capital data is collected, reducing manual research time by 50-70%.34

Rather than replacing FactSet, AI is becoming a reason to *subscribe* to FactSet, as the company’s "curated" data is the necessary fuel for any firm’s proprietary AI models. FactSet’s "open platform" strategy allows clients to integrate FactSet data directly into their own AI applications, making the company a critical infrastructure provider for the "algorithmic" age of finance.13

### **B. Segment Growth and TAM Expansion**

FactSet is successfully diversifying its revenue base beyond its core buy-side equity research roots.

* **Wealth Management:** ASV grew 10% in fiscal 2025, reaching $374 million.1 This segment is less saturated than institutional asset management and offers a vast "white space" for growth.
* **Market Infrastructure:** This segment, which includes CUSIP and other identifier services, grew 7-8% in fiscal 2025\.13 It provides a defensive, non-discretionary revenue stream.
* **Asia Pacific Expansion:** Organic ASV in the Asia-Pac region grew 8.4% in late 2025, highlighting the success of the company’s international expansion strategy.5

## **5\. Risk Assessment: Identifying the Tail Risks**

A deep value thesis must be stress-tested against the possibility of permanent capital loss. FactSet faces several challenges that could impair its long-term value.

### **A. Business and Technological Risks**

* **Technological Disruption (AI):** While AI is currently a tool for FactSet, it also lowers the barriers to entry for new competitors. If an "open source" financial data platform emerges that can match FactSet’s data quality at a fraction of the cost, the company’s pricing power would be severely compromised.26
* **Customer Consolidation:** The asset management industry is undergoing significant consolidation (e.g., large-scale mergers of banks and investment firms). This often leads to "vendor consolidation," where the merged entity chooses one platform and cancels the other. Given Bloomberg’s dominance, FactSet is sometimes on the losing side of these rationalizations.13
* **Budget Scrutiny:** Financial institutions are under intense pressure to reduce costs. Terminal subscriptions are a highly visible line item. Helen Shan, FactSet's CFO, noted that clients are becoming more strategic and "selective," leading to longer sales cycles and more rigorous approval processes.13

### **B. Financial and Macroeconomic Risks**

* **Cyclicality of Capital Markets:** While FactSet’s revenue is recurring, its *growth* is tied to the health of the financial sector. A prolonged bear market or a recession that leads to mass layoffs in investment banking would stifle new ASV growth.
* **Interest Rate Sensitivity:** While FactSet has reduced its debt, it still carries $1.37 billion in obligations. If interest rates remain "higher for longer," the cost of refinancing future maturities could weigh on net income.11
* **Currency Exposure:** Approximately one-third of FactSet’s revenue comes from outside the Americas. A significantly stronger US Dollar acts as a headwind to reported revenue and earnings growth.5

### **C. ESG and Governance Factors**

* **Governance:** The recent leadership transition and the high compensation package for the new CEO (including a $49 million "make-whole" and initial award) have drawn some scrutiny.29 However, the board's independence and the alignment of executive incentives with stockholder value through stock-based compensation remain generally favorable.27
* **Social/Labor:** FactSet relies on a global workforce, including large operations centers in India and the Philippines. Any geopolitical instability in these regions or changes in local labor laws could disrupt the company’s low-cost service model.

### **D. Tail Risks: The "Black Swan" Scenario**

What could cause a 50%+ permanent loss of capital?

1. **A massive data breach:** If FactSet’s proprietary data or client information were compromised, the resulting loss of trust could lead to a mass exodus of clients that even high switching costs could not prevent.
2. **Regulatory mandate for "Free" CUSIPs:** If regulators determined that security identifiers should be a public good and forced FactSet to provide CUSIP data for free, a significant portion of the company’s high-margin "Market Infrastructure" revenue would vanish.

A critical component of the Deep Value Stock Research Protocol is the investigation of warning signs that could indicate underlying rot.

### **Financial Red Flags**

* **Declining gross/operating margins without explanation?** NO. GAAP margins have dipped, but the company has been transparent about CUSIP amortization and AI investment cycles.5 **PASS**.
* **Accounts receivable growing faster than sales?** NO. Accounts receivable reached $252.5 million in late 2024, representing approximately 10% of annual revenue, which is consistent with historical norms and client growth.4 **PASS**.
* **Frequent restatements of financial results?** NO. There have been no significant restatements in the last five years.37 **PASS**.
* **Cash flow significantly diverging from earnings?** NO. Free cash flow of $617.5M is higher than net income of $597M.3 **PASS**.

### **Management Red Flags**

* **Heavy insider selling by multiple executives?** YES/NO. There has been significant selling by the outgoing CEO and other executives, totaling 16 sales in 6 months.42 However, this is largely attributed to retirement and diversification. Notably, the CFO and CRO have made opportunistic *purchases* in late 2025\.42 **PASS** (with monitoring).
* **High executive turnover?** NO. The CEO transition was a planned retirement after a 30-year career.29 **PASS**.
* **Promotional/misleading communication?** NO. FactSet management is known for its conservative, data-driven approach to guidance.13 **PASS**.

### **Business Red Flags**

* **Loss of major customers?** NO. Client count increased by 9.5% in fiscal 2025\.10 **PASS**.
* **Regulatory investigations/significant litigation?** NO. No material pending litigation or regulatory actions that threaten the core business model.27 **PASS**.

## **7\. Final Deliverables and Investment Thesis**

### **1\. Executive Summary**

FactSet Research Systems Inc. (FDS) presents a classic "value in plain sight" opportunity. For over a decade, the company was the darling of growth-at-a-reasonable-price (GARP) investors, consistently commanding a P/E multiple between 25x and 35x due to its impenetrable "moat," 95% retention rates, and multi-decade track record of earnings growth. Today, the market has de-rated the stock to 12.7x earnings—a multiple typically reserved for cyclical retailers or declining industrial firms.

This de-rating appears to be driven by "AI anxiety"—the fear that artificial intelligence will decimate the financial analyst profession. However, a deep dive into FactSet’s operations reveals that the company is successfully co-opting AI to increase its value proposition. FactSet’s "AigenTeq" and AI Doc Ingest tools are making analysts more productive, not obsolete, and the company is seeing its strongest growth in segments like Wealth Management and Asia Pacific.

The fundamental health of the business is exemplary: 25%+ net margins, a 15x interest coverage ratio, and free cash flow that consistently exceeds net income. With the stock trading at a 58% discount to its 10-year historical average and offering a 2.14% dividend yield (supported by a $1 billion share buyback program), the margin of safety for a value investor is substantial.

**Key Strengths:**

* **Irreplaceable Infrastructure:** Integration into the workflows of 9,000+ financial firms.
* **Monopoly Assets:** Control of CUSIP identifier data for North America.
* **Exceptional Cash Conversion:** FCF margin of 26%+ of revenue.
* **Dividend Aristocrat Trajectory:** 26 consecutive years of increases with a low payout ratio.

**Key Weaknesses:**

* **AI Disruption Uncertainty:** Potential for reduced "user counts" if firms downsize analysts.
* **Valuation Momentum:** The stock is currently in a downward trend as part of a sector-wide re-evaluation.
* **Margin Pressure:** Temporary compression as the company invests in its AI infrastructure.

### **2\. Investment Recommendation**

**Final Recommendation: BUY**

FactSet is a high-quality "moat" business trading at a "no-growth" price. For the patient investor, the combination of multiple expansion (reverting toward 18-22x), steady 5-7% revenue growth, and aggressive share buybacks creates a path to significant outperformance. The current price of \~$205 offers a rare entry point into a company that has compounded earnings for 29 consecutive years.

### **3\. Key Investment Risks (Top 3\)**

1. **Workforce Contraction:** If AI leads to a permanent 20%+ reduction in the number of human financial analysts, FactSet’s workstation revenue will face a headwind that its data-feed growth may struggle to offset.
2. **Competitive Pricing Pressure:** As AI tools lower the cost of basic data collection, FactSet may be forced to lower prices or increase its "managed services" at lower margins to maintain its user base.
3. **Refinancing Risk in a High-Rate Environment:** While the current leverage is manageable, any future large-scale acquisitions at high interest rates could impair the company’s ability to maintain its high ROE.

### **4\. Catalysts & Timeline**

* **Earnings Consistency (Next 2-4 Quarters):** Continued 5-6% organic ASV growth in a difficult macro environment will eventually force the market to re-evaluate the "death of the terminal" narrative.
* **Buyback Execution (2026):** The deployment of the $1 billion repurchase authorization will provide a significant floor for the share price and drive EPS growth even if net income growth remains modest.
* **AI Monetization Proof-Points (Fall 2026):** As management reports specific revenue contributions from AI-enhanced tools like Pitch Creator and AI Doc Ingest, the bear case will likely lose its potency.

### **5\. Comparison to Alternatives**

Within the "Diversified Financials" and "Capital Markets" industry, FactSet is the clear value leader.

* **vs. S\&P Global (SPGI):** SPGI is arguably a superior business due to its ratings oligopoly, but it trades at 27.4x earnings—more than double the price of FactSet. FactSet offers a significantly better risk-reward profile for a value-oriented investor.
* **vs. MSCI Inc.:** MSCI has slightly higher growth potential in its ESG and Climate segments, but its 31.7x multiple leaves zero room for error. FactSet provides a massive margin of safety.
* **vs. Morningstar (MORN):** Morningstar has a weaker moat and lower operating margins (15% vs FactSet’s 32%) yet trades at a higher P/E of 18x. FactSet is the higher-quality business at a lower price.

In conclusion, FactSet Research Systems represents a quintessential "Ben Graham" investment for the 21st century: a company with robust earnings, a clear competitive advantage, and a stock price that is currently an outlier in its historical and peer-group context. The market’s fixation on the potential disruption of AI has created a valuation vacuum that provides an exceptional opportunity for long-term capital appreciation.
